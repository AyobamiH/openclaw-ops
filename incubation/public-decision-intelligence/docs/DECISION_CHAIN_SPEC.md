# Decision Chain Specification

## Purpose

A decision chain explains how a material outcome formed over time.

It should connect:

- precursor signals
- deliberation events
- formal process events
- outcome events
- after-effects

## Decision Subject

Every chain needs a clear subject, such as:

- appointment
- funding decision
- policy shift
- committee recommendation

## Stage Model

### Precursor

Early conversations, introductions, lobbying, agenda-setting, or informal
signals that appear to begin the chain.

### Deliberation

Internal discussions, objections, draft language, briefings, or recommendation
activity.

### Formal Process

Committee review, approvals, parliamentary steps, official memos, or other
institutional checkpoints.

### Outcome

The final decision or formal announcement.

### After-Effects

Follow-up correspondence, implementation, media response, or later challenges.

## Uncertainty Handling

Decision chains must show:

- evidenced stages
- inferred stages
- gaps where chronology or causality is unresolved
- alternative interpretations where the chain is contested

## Linking Rules

A stage should only enter a chain if it has one or more of:

- direct citations
- strong inferred linkage to cited events or claims
- explicit reviewer acceptance

## Confidence And Review

Chain-level confidence is not a substitute for per-step evidence.

Review should be possible at:

- whole-chain level
- individual step level
- linkage or ordering level
